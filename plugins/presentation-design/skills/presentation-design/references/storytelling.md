# Storytelling for Presentations

A reference guide for structuring narrative in slide decks. Read this when planning the narrative arc during Phase 1 (Deck Planning) of the presentation-design skill.

---

## The Core Principle

Every presentation answers one question for the audience: **"Why should I care?"**

If a slide doesn't move the audience closer to caring, acting, or understanding — it shouldn't exist. Storytelling is the mechanism that connects your content to the audience's world.

---

## Deck-Level Narrative Structures

Choose the structure that fits your purpose. These are starting frameworks, not rigid templates — adapt them to your content.

### Situation → Complication → Resolution (SCR)

Best for: proposals, recommendations, change initiatives.

1. **Situation**: Establish shared reality. "Here's where we are." The audience should nod along — this is common ground.
2. **Complication**: Introduce the tension. "But here's the problem." This is where stakes emerge — something is at risk, broken, or changing.
3. **Resolution**: Present the path forward. "Here's what we should do." This earns its weight because the audience now feels the problem.

Why it works: humans are wired to seek resolution after tension. By establishing the complication before the solution, the audience is primed to receive your recommendation.

### The Minto Pyramid (Answer First)

Best for: executive updates, consulting recommendations, status reports, any audience that's short on time.

1. **Lead with the answer** — your recommendation, conclusion, or key finding goes on the first content slide. Don't build up to it.
2. **Supporting arguments** — each major argument gets its own section (2–4 slides).
3. **Evidence** — data, examples, and proof points that back each argument.

Why it works: busy audiences (executives, clients) want the "so what" immediately. They'll engage with the supporting detail only if the top-line conclusion interests them. This structure respects their time and mirrors how they make decisions.

Tip: if you find yourself saying "bear with me, I'm getting to the point" — you need Minto.

### What → So What → Now What

Best for: data presentations, research readouts, quarterly reviews.

1. **What**: Present the facts, data, or findings. Keep it descriptive, not interpretive.
2. **So What**: Explain why it matters. What are the implications? What changed? What's at risk?
3. **Now What**: Propose action. Given what we know and why it matters, what should we do?

Why it works: it separates observation from interpretation from action, which prevents the common failure mode of jumping from data to recommendations without explaining the connection.

### Problem → Approach → Evidence → Ask

Best for: pitch decks, funding requests, project proposals.

1. **Problem**: Define the pain point clearly. Make the audience feel it.
2. **Approach**: Present your solution or method. Keep it high-level — how, not implementation detail.
3. **Evidence**: Show proof it works — data, case studies, prototypes, testimonials.
4. **Ask**: State what you need — budget, approval, resources, a decision.

### Before → After → Bridge

Best for: sales presentations, product demos, transformation stories.

1. **Before**: Paint the current painful state. Use specific, relatable details.
2. **After**: Show the desired future state. Make it vivid and concrete.
3. **Bridge**: Reveal how to get from Before to After — your product, service, plan, or recommendation.

### Context → Options → Recommendation

Best for: decision decks, architecture reviews, vendor comparisons.

1. **Context**: Why are we making this decision now? What constraints exist?
2. **Options**: Present 2–4 viable paths. For each: brief description, pros, cons, rough cost/effort.
3. **Recommendation**: State which option you recommend and why. Don't be neutral — take a position.

## Opening Your Deck

The first 30 seconds determine whether your audience leans in or checks out. Your title slide and first content slide carry disproportionate weight.

### Hook Patterns

- **The surprising stat**: Lead with a number that challenges assumptions. "87% of strategy initiatives fail in the first year."
- **The question**: Pose a question the audience can't immediately answer. "What would it cost us to delay this decision by six months?"
- **The tension**: State a contradiction or problem directly. "We're growing revenue 30% year-over-year — and losing money on every new customer."
- **The story**: Start with a brief, specific anecdote. "Last Tuesday, a customer called support three times in four hours for the same issue."
- **The "what if"**: Invite the audience into a hypothetical. "What if we could cut onboarding time from six weeks to six days?"

### What NOT to Do

- Don't open with an agenda slide (nobody cares about your table of contents — they care about why they're here)
- Don't open with company background (the audience already knows who you are, or they'll find out as you speak)
- Don't open with definitions (if you need to define terms, do it inline when the terms first matter)

If you must include an agenda, put it on slide 2 or 3, after you've earned attention.

## Building Tension and Stakes

The middle of your deck is where most presentations die. The audience understood your opening, they'll perk up for your conclusion — but the middle is where attention drifts.

### The "So What" Test

For every slide in the middle of your deck, ask: **"If I removed this slide, would the audience's understanding or decision change?"** If the answer is no, remove it.

### Techniques for Sustaining Attention

- **Contrast**: Before/after, old way/new way, us/competitors. Contrast creates visual and conceptual interest.
- **Specificity**: "Revenue increased" is forgettable. "Revenue increased 23% in Q3, driven entirely by the APAC expansion" is memorable. Specific details signal credibility.
- **The gap**: Repeatedly highlight the distance between where things are and where they could be. Each slide should widen or narrow this gap.
- **One idea per slide**: The moment a slide tries to make two points, both get diluted. Split them.
- **Progressive disclosure**: Don't show everything at once. Build up to complex diagrams or data by introducing elements one layer at a time across multiple slides.

## Closing Your Deck

The last slide is what the audience remembers. Don't waste it on "Thank You" or "Questions?"

### Strong Closing Patterns

- **The callback**: Return to your opening hook with new context. If you opened with a problem, close by showing it solved. If you opened with a question, close with the answer.
- **The clear ask**: State exactly what you want from the audience. "I'm asking for $150K to fund a three-month pilot." Specificity shows confidence.
- **The next step**: Tell the audience what happens next. "If you approve today, we'll have a prototype by March 15." Remove ambiguity about the path forward.
- **The vision**: For inspirational or strategic decks, close with a vivid picture of the future state. Make it concrete enough to imagine.

### What NOT to End With

- "Thank You" as a standalone final slide (it signals "I'm done" without reinforcing your message)
- "Questions?" as a standalone final slide (your key message gets replaced by whatever random question comes last)
- A summary slide that repeats your agenda (you're recapping structure, not reinforcing impact)

Instead: put your closing message on the final slide, then take questions verbally. The last thing on screen should be your strongest point.

## Slide-Level Storytelling

Each slide is a micro-story. It should have:

1. **A headline that states the takeaway** — not a topic label. "Q3 Revenue Up 23%" is better than "Q3 Revenue." "Onboarding Takes Too Long" is better than "Onboarding Metrics."
2. **One key message** — if you can't summarize the slide's point in one sentence, it's doing too much.
3. **Supporting evidence** — the visual element (chart, image, diagram) or text that proves the headline.

### The Headline Test

Read only the headlines of your slides in sequence. If someone can follow your argument from headlines alone, your deck's narrative structure is strong. If the headlines read like a table of contents ("Background", "Methodology", "Results"), they're topic labels — rewrite them as assertions.

**Weak headlines** (topic labels):
- "Market Overview"
- "Customer Feedback"
- "Next Steps"

**Strong headlines** (assertions):
- "The Mid-Market Segment Is Underserved"
- "Customers Want Faster Onboarding, Not More Features"
- "Three Actions to Close the Gap by Q4"

## The Rule of Three

Human cognition gravitates toward groups of three. Use this throughout your deck:

- **Three key messages** for the overall deck
- **Three supporting points** per argument
- **Three items** in visual groupings (cards, columns, icons)

When you have four or more points, ask: can two of these be combined? If not, group them into three categories. If you truly need more than three, consider splitting across slides.

## Storytelling Anti-Patterns in Decks

- **The data dump**: Slides packed with charts and tables but no narrative connecting them. Data without interpretation is noise.
- **The mystery novel**: Building slowly to a reveal at the end. Executives will interrupt you on slide 3 asking for the punchline.
- **The wall of text**: Reading your slides to the audience. If your slides contain everything you're going to say, you're a narrator, not a presenter.
- **The orphan slide**: A slide that doesn't connect to what came before or after it. Every slide should earn its position in the sequence.
- **The false ending**: Saying "one more thing" or adding slides after what felt like the conclusion. If the audience mentally checked out, they won't check back in.
- **The hedge**: Softening every recommendation with caveats and disclaimers until the audience isn't sure what you're actually proposing. Take a position. Be clear. Address risks separately.
